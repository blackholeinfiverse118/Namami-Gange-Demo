# 2. Architecture Convergence

## 2.1 Marine MasterDB Foundation

The Marine MasterDB architecture serves as the foundational layer of the ecosystem.

It provides:

* Shared Ontology
* Shared Entity Definitions
* Shared Signal Vocabulary
* Shared Geospatial References
* Shared Programme Context
* Governance Rules
* Ownership Rules

All Sprint 6 deliverables align to this foundation.

The Marine MasterDB therefore functions as the common intelligence substrate supporting interoperability while preserving product independence.

---

## 2.2 Dataset Registry Alignment

The Marine Dataset Registry provides the intelligence source layer of the ecosystem.

The registry aligns with the Marine MasterDB ontology through:

* Shared Entities
* Shared Signal Families
* Shared Programme References
* Shared Classification Structures

The registry supplies the datasets required to generate:

* Signals
* Assessments
* Knowledge Artifacts
* Operational Intelligence

The registry therefore functions as the primary intelligence inventory supporting the ecosystem.

---

## 2.3 Chandragupta Alignment

The Chandragupta Payload Contract provides the intelligence delivery layer of the ecosystem.

Chandragupta consumes intelligence generated by:

* NICAI
* Namami Gange
* SVACS
* Shared Marine MasterDB

The payload architecture converts assessments, signals, interventions, and knowledge products into operationally meaningful control-surface views.

The relationship can be represented as:

```text
Marine MasterDB
        ↓
Assessments
        ↓
Payload Construction
        ↓
Chandragupta
        ↓
Human Operator
```

Chandragupta therefore functions as the operational intelligence presentation layer.

---

## 2.4 SVACS Alignment

SVACS provides the semantic and contextual intelligence layer of the ecosystem.

SVACS contributes:

* Historical Context
* Cultural Context
* Heritage Intelligence
* Sacred Geography Intelligence
* Civilizational Context
* Knowledge Artifacts

The introduction of the Knowledge Artifact Schema extends the Marine MasterDB schema family architecture and provides a formal persistence model for semantic intelligence.

The relationship can be represented as:

```text
Knowledge Sources
        ↓
Semantic Linking
        ↓
Knowledge Artifacts
        ↓
SVACS
        ↓
Shared Context
```

SVACS therefore enriches the ecosystem through contextual understanding while remaining independent from operational and ecological intelligence responsibilities.

---

## 2.5 Converged Architecture View

The complete Sprint 6 architecture can be summarized as:

```text
Datasets
      ↓

Signals / Knowledge Sources
      ↓

Assessments / Knowledge Artifacts
      ↓

Marine MasterDB
      ↓

Chandragupta
      ↓

Human Operator
```

This architecture establishes a clear separation between:

* Intelligence Sources
* Intelligence Processing
* Intelligence Persistence
* Intelligence Presentation
* Human Decision Making

while preserving governance, ownership, explainability, and interoperability requirements.

## 4.6 Dependency Governance Principles

Cross-product dependencies should preserve:

### Principle 1

Product Independence

Each product remains deployable independently.

---

### Principle 2

Ownership Independence

Artifacts remain owned by originating products.

---

### Principle 3

Reference-Based Integration

Products share references rather than direct ownership.

---

### Principle 4

Controlled Consumption

Consumers may read approved intelligence but may not alter originating artifacts.

---

### Principle 5

Architecture Stability

Changes within one subsystem should not require redesign of unrelated subsystems.

# 6. Risk Register

## 6.1 Data Risks

Data availability and data quality represent foundational risks for the Marine Intelligence ecosystem.

### Risk 1 — Dataset Incompleteness

Description:

Required datasets may be unavailable, fragmented, outdated, or geographically incomplete.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced intelligence quality.
* Reduced assessment confidence.
* Coverage gaps.

Mitigation:

* Dataset Registry governance.
* Gap identification processes.
* Future acquisition planning.

---

### Risk 2 — Dataset Consistency

Description:

Different datasets may use inconsistent classifications, identifiers, or reference systems.

Potential Impact:

* Ontology misalignment.
* Entity duplication.
* Integration complexity.

Mitigation:

* Shared ontology governance.
* Standardized entity references.
* Validation procedures.

---

### Risk 3 — Historical Source Quality

Description:

Historical and cultural sources may vary in reliability, completeness, or traceability.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced confidence in Knowledge Artifacts.
* Semantic inconsistencies.

Mitigation:

* Source Registry governance.
* Confidence attribution.
* Provenance preservation.

---

## 6.2 Operational Risks

Operational risks affect intelligence generation and decision-support capabilities.

### Risk 1 — Assessment Quality

Description:

Incorrect assumptions or weak source data may produce inaccurate assessments.

Potential Impact:

* Misleading operational visibility.
* Reduced operator trust.

Mitigation:

* Explainability controls.
* Confidence visibility.
* Human oversight.

---

### Risk 2 — Information Overload

Description:

Large numbers of entities, signals, and assessments may overwhelm operators.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced situational awareness.
* Slower decision making.

Mitigation:

* Priority Conditions Payloads.
* Recommended Actions Payloads.
* Operational filtering.

---

### Risk 3 — Ownership Ambiguity

Description:

Recommendations or interventions may lack clear ownership.

Potential Impact:

* Delayed response.
* Accountability gaps.

Mitigation:

* Human Ownership Payloads.
* Escalation visibility.
* Governance reviews.

---

## 6.3 Integration Risks

Integration risks affect interoperability between ecosystem components.

### Risk 1 — Ontology Drift

Description:

Subsystems may evolve independently and diverge from shared ontology standards.

Potential Impact:

* Interoperability degradation.
* Semantic inconsistency.

Mitigation:

* Ontology governance.
* Schema reviews.
* Architecture validation.

---

### Risk 2 — Contract Drift

Description:

Payload structures or schema definitions may evolve inconsistently across products.

Potential Impact:

* Integration failures.
* Data consumption issues.

Mitigation:

* Contract versioning.
* Schema validation.
* Change management reviews.

---

### Risk 3 — Dependency Expansion

Description:

Subsystems may become increasingly dependent on internal implementation details of other products.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced product independence.
* Increased maintenance complexity.

Mitigation:

* Reference-based interoperability.
* Ownership isolation.
* Stable interfaces.

---

## 6.4 Governance Risks

Governance risks affect trustworthiness and long-term sustainability.

### Risk 1 — Provenance Loss

Description:

Artifacts may lose traceability to supporting datasets or sources.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced explainability.
* Reduced auditability.

Mitigation:

* Mandatory provenance requirements.
* Validation checks.

---

### Risk 2 — Ownership Violations

Description:

Products may attempt to modify assets owned by other subsystems.

Potential Impact:

* Governance conflicts.
* Data integrity concerns.

Mitigation:

* Bucket isolation.
* Access controls.
* Governance enforcement.

---

### Risk 3 — Explainability Degradation

Description:

Complex intelligence products may become difficult to interpret.

Potential Impact:

* Reduced operator confidence.
* Reduced decision quality.

Mitigation:

* Explainability requirements.
* Provenance visibility.
* Human review workflows.

---

## 6.5 Risk Assessment Summary

The highest-priority risks identified during Sprint 6 are:

1. Dataset Availability Risk
2. Ontology Drift Risk
3. Contract Drift Risk
4. Provenance Loss Risk
5. Ownership Ambiguity Risk

These risks should receive priority attention during future implementation and deployment planning.
